I recently attended the third training school of BEiNG-WISE, “Cybersecurity and Human Factors from an Industrial and Ethical Perspective.”
The event was fantastic, bringing together diverse disciplines under one roof.
The key takeaway was clear: cybersecurity is not just technical, it is deeply human.
The program included insightful discussions on psychology, ethics, and law.

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/beingwise2_08_2025.jpg" title="BEiNG-WISE"  %}

The school began with a fascinating demo of the Flipper Zero by Marcin Czerniawski, whose contributions and questions enriched the sessions.(Thank you for the gift, Marcin.)

Obviously, artificial intelligence was among the hottest topics, with several talks exploring its promise and challenges.
Other sessions emphasized the importance of organizational culture, while one particularly striking presentation
showed how attackers exploit psychology; using cognitive biases and social dynamics to manipulate victims.

It reminded me that while technology keeps advancing, humans often remain the weakest link in the security chain.
However, with awareness, training, and a strong cybersecurity culture, this weakness can be transformed into resilience.

The legal dimension of cybersecurity also received strong attention. Personally, I believe legislation in both cybersecurity and AI remains
underdeveloped. That is why BEiNG-WISE has established a dedicated working group, WP5: Legal Factors in Cybersecurity Wireless Systems: 
A Vertical Approach, which aims to address these challenges and bridge the gap between technology and policy.

I also had the opportunity to present our own research from the HI-ACT project, where we focus on the cybersecurity of hydrogen systems.
My talk centered on our progress in generating benchmarking datasets for intrusion detection systems. The lack of existing labelled dataset for hydrogen
systems impedes the development of IDS. Therefore, this work supports the development of stronger defenses against cyberattacks in clean energy infrastructure.

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/beingwise_08_2025.jpg" title="BEiNG-WISE"  %}

This was also my first COST Action event. COST is all about networking, and it not only introduced me to leading experts from across 
Europe but also gave me the chance to make new friends. I am truly grateful to the organizers for creating such a welcoming and inspiring environment.

Beyond the academic sessions, the event offered a much-needed personal break. 
Having traveled frequently for work, I was feeling exhausted, but spending time in Litochoro, Greece was both refreshing and motivating. The town’s natural beauty, combined with the intellectual energy of the school,
provided space to recharge, reflect, and enjoy moments outside the conference.

Overall, the BEiNG-WISE training school was a reminder that cybersecurity is a multidisciplinary effort where
 technology, humans, and laws intersect. I left with new insights, valuable collaborations, and renewed energy for my own research journey.
